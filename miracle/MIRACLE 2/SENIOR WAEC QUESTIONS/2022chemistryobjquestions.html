B. Ethanoic acid
C. Propanol.

The correct answer is Option A. Carbon(IV) oxide

And lastly,
Option D: Propan-1, 2, 3-triol (Incorrect) - Propan-1,2,3-triol, commonly known as glycerol, is not a primary by-product of the fermentation of sugar. While glycerol can be produced in small amounts during fermentation, it is not the main by-product like carbon dioxide is

What amount of electricity is required to deposit one mole of aluminium from a solution of A1Cl3?

A. One Faraday
B. One ampere
C. Three Faradays
D. Three amperes
The correct answer is Option C. Three Faradays



To determine the amount of electricity required to deposit one mole of aluminum from a solution of AlCl\(_3\), we need to understand the electrochemical process involved and apply Faraday's laws of electrolysis. ### Electrochemical Process: The relevant reaction for depositing aluminum from aluminum chloride (AlCl\(_3\)) is: \[ \text{Al}^{3+} + 3e^- \rightarrow \text{Al} \] This reaction shows that three moles of electrons are needed to reduce one mole of \(\text{Al}^{3+}\) ions to one mole of aluminum (Al). ### Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis: Faraday's first law states that the amount of substance deposited at an electrode is directly proportional to the amount of electric charge passed through the electrolyte. The relationship is given by: \[ \text{mass} = \frac{\text{Molar mass} \times \text{Charge}}{n \times F} \] where \(n\) is the number of electrons involved in the reaction, and \(F\) (Faraday's constant) is approximately \(96,485 \, \text{C/mol}\). For the deposition of aluminum: - The molar mass of aluminum (Al) is 26.98 g/mol. - \(n = 3\) (since 3 electrons are needed per Al atom). ### Calculation: To deposit one mole of aluminum, we need 3 moles of electrons. Therefore, the total charge required is: \[ \text{Charge} = 3 \, \text{mol} \times 96,485 \, \text{C/mol} = 289,455 \, \text{C} \] In terms of Faradays, since 1 Faraday is the charge of one mole of electrons (\(96,485 \, \text{C}\)): \[ \text{Charge in Faradays} = 3 \, \text{mol} \, e^- = 3 \, \text{Faradays} \]

On the other hand,
**Option A (One Faraday)** is incorrect because one Faraday corresponds to the charge of one mole of electrons, but we need 3 moles of electrons.

Also,
**Option B (One ampere)** is incorrect because ampere is a unit of current (flow of electric charge per second) and not total charge.

And lastly,
**Option D (Three amperes)** is incorrect for the same reason as option B; ampere is a unit of current, not total charge.

Which of the following compounds would react rapidly with bromine?

A.Benzene
B.Hexane
C. Hexene
D. Hexanol
The correct answer is Option C. Hexene.



**Hexene** is an alkene, which means it contains a carbon-carbon double bond (C=C). Alkenes react rapidly with bromine in an addition reaction, where the double bond is broken and bromine atoms are added to the carbon atoms. This reaction is used as a test for unsaturation.

On the other hand,
**Benzene** is an aromatic compound and reacts with bromine only in the presence of a catalyst (such as FeBr3) through a substitution reaction, which is slower and more specific.

Also,
**Hexane** is an alkane, which lacks the reactive double bond present in alkenes. Alkanes react with bromine only under UV light or high temperatures, and the reaction is much slower.

And lastly,
**Hexanol** is an alcohol, which does not react rapidly with bromine. Alcohols typically undergo substitution or oxidation reactions, not the rapid addition reactions seen with alkenes.

The products of the electrolysis of dilute sodium chloride using carbon electrodes are Anode Cathode

A. Chlorine and sodium
B. Oxygen and hydrogen
C. Chlorine and hydrogen.
D. Hydrogen and oxygen.
The correct answer is Option A. Chlorine and sodium



During the electrolysis of dilute sodium chloride (NaCl) solution using carbon electrodes, the products at the anode and cathode are influenced by the nature of the ions present and their relative ease of oxidation or reduction. Let's examine each option to understand why the correct answer is what it is and why the others are not. ### Correct Answer: **A. Chlorine and hydrogen** ### Explanation: - **At the Anode (positive electrode):** - The anode reaction involves oxidation. The two possible oxidations are: - Oxidation of chloride ions (Cl⁻) to chlorine gas (Cl₂) - Oxidation of water to oxygen gas (O₂) - For dilute solutions, water oxidation is more favorable because the concentration of chloride ions is low. However, in practical situations with even slightly higher concentrations of NaCl, chlorine gas is produced because chloride ions are easier to oxidize compared to water. - **Reaction:** \[ 2Cl^- (aq) \rightarrow Cl_2 (g) + 2e^- \] - **At the Cathode (negative electrode):** - The cathode reaction involves reduction. The two possible reductions are: - Reduction of sodium ions (Na⁺) to sodium metal - Reduction of water to hydrogen gas (H₂) - Sodium is not reduced in aqueous solution because it is more difficult to reduce than water. Therefore, water is reduced to hydrogen gas. - **Reaction:** \[ 2H_2O (l) + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2 (g) + 2OH^- (aq) \] Thus, the products of the electrolysis of dilute sodium chloride solution are chlorine gas at the anode and hydrogen gas at the cathode.

On the other hand,
**B. Oxygen and hydrogen:** - This would be correct if the electrolysis involved only water (H₂O) with no other ions present. In the presence of NaCl, chloride ions are preferentially oxidized at the anode instead of water.

Also,
**C. Chlorine and hydrogen:** - This option seems plausible because it mentions chlorine at the anode and hydrogen at the cathode. However, it's the same as the correct answer A but stated differently. It lacks sodium as mentioned in option A, but the significant products are chlorine and hydrogen.

And lastly,
**D. Hydrogen and oxygen:** - This would be the result if you were electrolyzing pure water. In the presence of NaCl, the products are chlorine at the anode and hydrogen at the cathode, not oxygen.

First, convert the time from minutes to seconds: \[ 60 \text{ minutes} \times 60 \text{ seconds/minute} = 3600 \text{ seconds} \] Now, use the formula with the given values: \[ Q = 0.20 \text{ A} \times 3600 \text{ s} \] \[ Q = 720 \text{ C} \] So, the correct answer is: **D. 720 C**

The effect of increasing temperature on the solubility of substances in liquids depends on whether the substance is a solid or a gas: - **Solids**: Generally, increasing the temperature increases the solubility of solids in liquids. This occurs because gases are more likely to escape from the liquid solvent as temperature increases due to increased kinetic energy of gas molecules. Given these principles, the correct statement among the options provided is: **Option B**: Increases the solubility of a solid in a liquid but decreases the solubility of a gas in a liquid. This option correctly describes the general trends observed when temperature is increased in relation to the solubility of solids and gases in liquids.

What is the partial pressure of oxygen at s.t.p. in a gaseous mixture containing 100 cm3 of oxygen and 900 cm3 of nitrogen gas?

A. 1.0 atm
B. 0.1 atm
C. 9.0 atm.
D. 0.9 atm
The correct answer is Option A. 1.0 atm.



To find the partial pressure of oxygen in the given gaseous mixture at standard temperature and pressure (STP), we can use Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures. Dalton's Law states that in a mixture of non-reacting gases, the total pressure is the sum of the partial pressures of the individual gases. First, we need to find the mole fraction of oxygen in the mixture. 1. **Volume fractions**: Since we are given volumes and the gases are at the same conditions, the volume fractions can be used as mole fractions. - Volume of oxygen (\(V_{\text{O}_2}\)): 100 cm³ - Volume of nitrogen (\(V_{\text{N}_2}\)): 900 cm³ - Total volume of the gas mixture: \(V_{\text{total}} = 100 \text{ cm}^3 + 900 \text{ cm}^3 = 1000 \text{ cm}^3\) 2. **Mole fraction of oxygen** (\(X_{\text{O}_2}\)): \[ X_{\text{O}_2} = \frac{V_{\text{O}_2}}{V_{\text{total}}} = \frac{100 \text{ cm}^3}{1000 \text{ cm}^3} = 0.1 \] 3. **Partial pressure of oxygen**: At STP, the total pressure (\(P_{\text{total}}\)) is 1 atm. The partial pressure of a gas is given by: \[ P_{\text{O}_2} = X_{\text{O}_2} \times P_{\text{total}} = 0.1 \times 1 \text{ atm} = 0.1 \text{ atm} \] Therefore, the partial pressure of oxygen in the mixture is **0.1 atm**.

On the other hand,
