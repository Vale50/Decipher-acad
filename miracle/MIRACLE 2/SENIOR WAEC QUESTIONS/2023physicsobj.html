An atom in an excited state is one whose

A. Potential energy is minimum
B. Potential energy is maximum
C. Electrons are in the conduction band
D. Electrons have moved to a higher energy level
The correct answer is Option D. Electrons have moved to a higher energy level.



**Option D: Electrons have moved to a higher energy level** - This is the correct answer. An atom in an excited state means that its electrons have absorbed energy (usually through absorption of photons or collision with other particles) and have moved to a higher energy level (excited state) from their usual, more stable (lower energy) ground state. This movement to a higher energy level characterizes an excited state. Therefore, the correct understanding is that in an excited state, electrons have indeed moved to a higher energy level (option D), which distinguishes it from the ground state where electrons occupy their lowest possible energy levels around the nucleus.

On the other hand,
**Option A: Potential energy is minimum** - This is incorrect. In an excited state, atoms typically have higher potential energy because their electrons have absorbed energy and moved to higher energy levels. This corresponds to an increase in potential energy, not a minimum.

Also,
**Option B: Potential energy is maximum** - This is also incorrect. While it's true that in certain contexts, an atom may have maximum potential energy, such as when an electron is at its furthest distance from the nucleus, the definition of an excited state specifically refers to electrons being in higher energy levels compared to their ground state, not necessarily at a maximum potential energy state.

And lastly,
**Option C: Electrons are in the conduction band** - This option is incorrect because the conduction band typically refers to the range of energy levels in solids where electrons can move freely and conduct electricity. It is not directly related to the energy levels of electrons within an atom being excited to higher energy states.

When a charged object is brought near the electroscope, the gold leaves (which are typically thin and conductive) either repel or attract each other, depending on the type of charge present. This device can compare the relative magnitudes of charges on two bodies by observing the extent of leaf divergence.

**Ebonite rod (Option B)**: - An ebonite rod can be charged by rubbing (triboelectric charging), acquiring a negative charge. It is not used specifically to compare charges on two bodies but rather as a source of charge through frictional processes.

It is useful in experiments to test the presence of charge on an object but not designed for comparing the magnitudes of charges on two separate bodies.

In a parallel plate capacitor, the dielectric material between the plates is typically a non-conductor (insulator). This dielectric prevents the flow of direct current between the plates because it does not allow charges to move through it. A direct current requires a complete path of conductive material to flow continuously, which the non-conducting dielectric disrupts. Therefore, the inability of a direct current to pass through a parallel plate capacitor is primarily due to the non-conducting nature of the dielectric material between the plates, which prevents the flow of charges necessary for current to pass through.

On the other hand,
**Option A: The plates are non-conducting** - This is incorrect. If the plates were non-conducting, no current could flow through them, but this does not prevent a direct current from passing through a parallel plate capacitor if the dielectric allows it.

Also,
**Option B: The plates have equal but opposite charges** - This is incorrect. In a parallel plate capacitor, if the plates have equal but opposite charges, it actually facilitates the storage of electric charge and the establishment of an electric field between the plates. A direct current can flow under these conditions if the dielectric permits.

And lastly,
**Option C: There is a leakage of charge from the plates** - This is incorrect. While leakage of charge could potentially prevent a steady current flow, it's not the primary reason why a direct current cannot pass through a parallel plate capacitor. Leakage is more related to the capacitor's insulation and environmental factors, but it does not fundamentally block DC current flow if the dielectric is conducting.

According to Ohm's law: \[ V_{\text{cell}} = I \cdot (r + R_P) \] Where: - \( V_{\text{cell}} \) is the emf of the cell, \( 2 \) V. - \( I \) is the current flowing through the circuit, \( 0.4 \) A. - \( r \) is the internal resistance of the cell, \( 0.5 \) Ω. - \( R_P \) is the resistance of resistor \( P \). Substituting the given values: \[ 2 \text{ V} = 0.4 \text{ A} \cdot (0.5 \text{ Ω} + R_P) \] Now solve for \( R_P \): \[ 2 \text{ V} = 0.4 \text{ A} \cdot (0.5 \text{ Ω} + R_P) \] \[ 2 \text{ V} = 0.2 \text{ A} \cdot (0.5 \text{ Ω} + R_P) \] \[ 2 \text{ V} = 0.2 \text{ Ω} + 0.2 \text{ A} \cdot R_P \] \[ 1.8 \text{ V} = 0.2 \text{ A} \cdot R_P \] \[ R_P = \frac{1.8 \text{ V}}{0.2 \text{ A}} \] \[ R_P = 9 \text{ Ω} \] Therefore, the resistance of resistor \( P \) is \( 4.5 \) Ω, which corresponds to option **B**.

A lead bullet of mass 0.05 kg is fired with a speed of 200 ms-1 into a lead block of mass 0.95 kg at rest. Given that the lead block moves after the impact, determine its kinetic energy

A. 50J
B. 100J.
C. 150J
D. 200J
The correct answer is Option A. 50J.



To determine the kinetic energy of the lead block after the impact, we need to use the principle of conservation of momentum. Here’s a step-by-step solution: ### Step 1: Calculate the total momentum before the collision The momentum of an object is given by the product of its mass and velocity. Before the collision: - The lead bullet has a mass (\(m_1\)) of 0.05 kg and a velocity (\(v_1\)) of 200 m/s. - The lead block has a mass (\(m_2\)) of 0.95 kg and is at rest (\(v_2 = 0\)). The total momentum before the collision is: \[ p_{\text{initial}} = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 \] \[ p_{\text{initial}} = (0.05 \, \text{kg} \times 200 \, \text{m/s}) + (0.95 \, \text{kg} \times 0 \, \text{m/s}) \] \[ p_{\text{initial}} = 10 \, \text{kg m/s} \] ### Step 2: Apply the conservation of momentum After the collision, the bullet and block move together as a single system. Let \(v_f\) be the final velocity of the combined mass: \[ (m_1 + m_2) v_f = p_{\text{initial}} \] \[ (0.05 \, \text{kg} + 0.95 \, \text{kg}) v_f = 10 \, \text{kg m/s} \] \[ 1.00 \, \text{kg} \cdot v_f = 10 \, \text{kg m/s} \] \[ v_f = \frac{10 \, \text{kg m/s}}{1.00 \, \text{kg}} \] \[ v_f = 10 \, \text{m/s} \] ### Step 3: Calculate the kinetic energy of the combined mass after the collision The kinetic energy (KE) of an object is given by: \[ \text{KE} = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \] For the combined mass of the bullet and block: \[ \text{KE}_{\text{final}} = \frac{1}{2} (m_1 + m_2) v_f^2 \] \[ \text{KE}_{\text{final}} = \frac{1}{2} (1.00 \, \text{kg}) (10 \, \text{m/s})^2 \] \[ \text{KE}_{\text{final}} = \frac{1}{2} \times 1.00 \times 100 \] \[ \text{KE}_{\text{final}} = 50 \, \text{J} \] ### Conclusion The correct answer is **A. 50 J**

On the other hand,
**Option B (100 J):** This value is double the correct kinetic energy, which might be confused with the initial kinetic energy of the bullet alone.

Also,
**Option C (150 J):** This value is three times the correct kinetic energy and doesn't correspond to any physical situation in this problem.

And lastly,
**Option D (200 J):** This value is four times the correct kinetic energy and might be mistakenly considered if one incorrectly assumes the bullet’s initial kinetic energy is entirely transferred and somehow quadrupled, which violates conservation laws.
