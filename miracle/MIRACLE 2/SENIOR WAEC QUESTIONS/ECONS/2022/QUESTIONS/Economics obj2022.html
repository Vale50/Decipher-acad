2.

There is unemployment of resources when production is

A. Within the production possibility curve.
B. Outside the production possibility curve
C. Along the production possibility curve
D. Adequate to meet market demand
The correct answer is Option A. Within the production possibility curve.



A. Within the production possibility curve (Correct Answer): The production possibility curve (PPC) represents the maximum possible output combinations of two goods or services that can be produced with available resources and technology, assuming full and efficient use of those resources. If production is within the PPC, it indicates that the economy is not utilizing all available resources efficiently. This could be due to unemployment of labor, underutilization of capital, or other inefficiencies, leading to unemployment of resources.

On the other hand,
B. Outside the production possibility curve (Incorrect): Production outside the PPC is not possible with the current resources and technology. It represents a level of production that the economy cannot achieve unless there is an increase in resources or an improvement in technology. Therefore, it does not relate to unemployment of resources.

Also,
C. Along the production possibility curve (Incorrect): Production along the PPC indicates that resources are being used efficiently and the economy is operating at its full potential. All resources are fully employed, so there is no unemployment of resources.

And lastly,
D. Adequate to meet market demand (Incorrect): This option is unrelated to the concept of the PPC. Meeting market demand depends on various factors such as consumer preferences, prices, and production decisions. It does not directly address whether resources are fully employed or not.
3.

A major characteristic of natural resources is that they

A. Are unlimited in supply
B. Have high cost of production
C. Are free gifts of nature
D. Do not command any price.
The correct answer is Option C. Are free gifts of nature



Correct Answer (C. Are free gifts of nature): Natural resources are considered "free gifts of nature" because they exist in the environment without human intervention. Examples include water, air, minerals, and forests. These resources are provided by nature and are available for human use without any direct cost of creation.

On the other hand,
A. Are unlimited in supply: This is incorrect because natural resources are not unlimited. Many natural resources, like fossil fuels and minerals, are finite and can be exhausted if overused. Renewable resources like water and forests can also become scarce if not managed sustainably.

Also,
B. Have high cost of production: This is incorrect because natural resources themselves do not have a cost of production. They are naturally occurring and do not require human effort to be produced. However, the extraction, processing, and transportation of these resources may involve costs.

And lastly,
D. Do not command any price: This is incorrect because natural resources can command a price in the market. For example, oil, gold, and timber are sold at specific prices. The market value of these resources depends on their demand, availability, and the cost associated with extracting or refining them.

6.

The increase in the demand for a commodity may lead to a decrease in the demand for another if both are

A. In complemented demand
B. Of the same quality
C. In composite demand
D. In competitive demand.
The correct answer is Option D. In competitive demand.



D. In competitive demand: Correct Answer: Competitive demand refers to the situation where two goods are substitutes for each other. An increase in the demand for one good typically leads to a decrease in the demand for the other because consumers will switch from one product to another if they find it more desirable or cheaper. For example, if the demand for tea increases, the demand for coffee might decrease if both are substitutes in the eyes of the consumer.

On the other hand,
A. In complemented demand: Incorrect: Complementary goods are goods that are typically used together, such as cars and gasoline. An increase in the demand for one usually leads to an increase in the demand for the other, not a decrease.

Also,
B. Of the same quality: Incorrect: The quality of the goods does not directly relate to the demand for one leading to a decrease in the demand for another. If two goods are of the same quality, they may be substitutes, but quality alone does not define the relationship between their demands.

And lastly,
C. In composite demand: Incorrect: Composite demand refers to a situation where a single good is demanded for multiple purposes. For example, crude oil is demanded for producing gasoline, plastics, and other products. An increase in the demand for one of its uses does not typically lead to a decrease in the demand for another use; rather, it might increase the overall demand for the good.

The demand curve for goods of ostentation is usually

A. Negatively sloped
B. Positively sloped
C. Vertical
D. Horizontal
The correct answer is Option B. Positively sloped



Correct Answer (B. Positively sloped): The demand curve for goods of ostentation, also known as Veblen goods, is usually positively sloped. These are goods where the demand increases as the price increases because higher prices make them more desirable as status symbols. The higher the price, the more prestigious the item is perceived, which increases its demand among certain consumers.

On the other hand,
A. Negatively sloped: A negatively sloped demand curve is typical for most goods, where demand decreases as the price increases. However, this does not apply to goods of ostentation, where higher prices can actually increase demand.

Also,
C. Vertical: A vertical demand curve suggests that the quantity demanded does not change regardless of the price. This would imply that consumers buy the same quantity of the good no matter the price, which is not the case for Veblen goods.

And lastly,
D. Horizontal: A horizontal demand curve would indicate that the price of the good does not change regardless of the quantity demanded, which is not applicable to Veblen goods. The demand for these goods is sensitive to changes in price, but in the opposite direction compared to normal goods.

In perfectly elastic supply, the supply curve

A. Is vertical.
B. Is horizontal.
C. Slopes upward
D. Slopes downward.
The correct answer is Option C. B. Is horizontal.



The correct answer is B. Is horizontal. Explanation: In perfectly elastic supply, the supply curve is horizontal. This means that the quantity supplied can increase or decrease without affecting the price. In other words, suppliers are willing to supply any amount of the good at a constant price. This characteristic defines perfectly elastic supply.

On the other hand,
A. Is vertical: A vertical supply curve represents perfectly inelastic supply, where the quantity supplied remains constant regardless of price changes. This is not the case for perfectly elastic supply.

Also,
C. Slopes upward: An upward-sloping supply curve represents positive elasticity of supply, where higher prices incentivize more supply. However, in perfectly elastic supply, the price remains constant, so the curve is horizontal, not upward-sloping.

And lastly,
D. Slopes downward: A downward-sloping supply curve would indicate negative elasticity, where higher prices lead to a decrease in quantity supplied. This does not describe perfectly elastic supply.

An increase in the price of a derived good typically affects the cost of producing final goods, not the supply of another commodity directly.

15.

If the government imposes a minimum price on a commodity

A. Market surplus occurs
B. The market will be cleared in the short-run.
C. Excess demand occurs.
D. Government regulation is no longer needed.
The correct answer is Option A. Market surplus occurs.



A. Market surplus occurs: This is correct because a price floor set above the equilibrium price leads to a market surplus. When the government sets a minimum price higher than what would be the equilibrium price (where supply equals demand), suppliers are willing to supply more of the commodity than consumers are willing to buy at that higher price, resulting in a surplus.

On the other hand,
B. The market will be cleared in the short-run: This is incorrect because a price floor set above the equilibrium price does not clear the market. Instead, it creates a surplus, as there is more supply than demand at that price level.

Also,
C. Excess demand occurs: This is incorrect because excess demand, or a shortage, occurs when a price ceiling (a maximum allowable price) is set below the equilibrium price, leading to more demand than supply. A price floor, on the other hand, creates a surplus, not a shortage.

And lastly,
D. Government regulation is no longer needed: This is incorrect because the imposition of a price floor indicates that government regulation is actually being used to influence market outcomes. The need for government regulation persists as the price floor disrupts the natural balance of supply and demand.

A. Price ceiling

Price Floor: A minimum price legislation is designed to set a lower limit on prices. This is known as a price floor. For example, a government might set a minimum price for a product to ensure that sellers receive a fair wage or to stabilize the market.

17.

Which of the following factors is not a cause of diminishing returns?

A. Increase in variable inputs
B. Land fragmentation
C. Constant technology
D. Technological innovations.
The correct answer is Option C. Constant technology



Diminishing returns occurs when adding more of a variable input (e.g., labor or capital) to a fixed input (e.g., land) results in smaller increases in output. This concept is important in economics and production. Constant technology (C): Having constant technology does not inherently cause diminishing returns. In fact, constant technology implies that the productivity of inputs remains the same, and changes in output are solely due to changes in the quantity of inputs. Diminishing returns are more directly related to the quantity and efficiency of inputs rather than the level of technology.

On the other hand,
Increase in variable inputs (A): This typically leads to diminishing returns when other inputs are held constant. As more variable inputs are added, the additional output produced from each extra unit of input tends to decrease because the fixed inputs become overutilized.

Also,
Land fragmentation (B): This can cause diminishing returns because dividing land into smaller plots can lead to less efficient use of resources. Smaller plots might be less productive due to reduced economies of scale and the inability to fully utilize the fixed input.

And lastly,
Technological innovations (D): Technological innovations can counteract diminishing returns by increasing productivity. Improved technology can make the use of inputs more efficient and increase output, thus mitigating the effects of diminishing returns.

19.

The production cost that varies inversely with output is the

A. Total fixed cost.
B. Marginal cost
C. Average fixed cost
D. Average cost
The correct answer is Option C. Average fixed cost.



Average Fixed Cost (AFC): Average fixed cost is the fixed cost per unit of output. Since fixed costs do not change with the level of output, as output increases, the average fixed cost decreases because the same total fixed cost is spread over more units. Thus, average fixed cost varies inversely with output.

On the other hand,
Total Fixed Cost (TFC): Total fixed cost remains constant regardless of the level of output. It does not vary with output, so it is not the correct answer.

Also,
Marginal Cost (MC): Marginal cost is the additional cost incurred from producing one more unit of output. It typically changes with the level of output and is not necessarily inversely related to output.

And lastly,
Average Cost (AC): Average cost is the total cost divided by the number of units produced (AC = TC/Q). It includes both fixed and variable costs. As output increases, average cost may decrease or increase depending on the behavior of both fixed and variable costs, so it does not necessarily vary inversely with output.

The sufficient condition for a firm to be in equilibrium is that the

A. Firm must show that it is profitable.
B. Marginal cost must be equal to average revenue.
C. Marginal revenue curve is above the average revenue curve
D. Marginal cost curve cuts the marginal revenue curve from below
The correct answer is Option D. Marginal cost curve cuts the marginal revenue curve from below.



In economic theory, a firm is in equilibrium when it maximizes its profit. This occurs when: Marginal Cost (MC) equals Marginal Revenue (MR): This condition ensures that the cost of producing an additional unit of output is equal to the revenue it generates, thereby maximizing profit. Marginal Cost curve cuts the Marginal Revenue curve from below: This specific condition ensures that the firm is operating in the profit-maximizing region. If the MC curve were above the MR curve at the point of intersection, it would mean that producing more would increase costs more than revenue, leading to a decrease in profit. Conversely, if MC cuts MR from below, the firm is producing at a level where additional units are still profitable.

On the other hand,
A. Firm must show that it is profitable: Profitability alone does not guarantee equilibrium. A firm can be profitable but not in equilibrium if it is not maximizing profit. Equilibrium is specifically about the point where MC equals MR.

Also,
B. Marginal cost must be equal to average revenue: This condition is not sufficient for equilibrium. Equilibrium requires MC to equal MR, not average revenue. Average revenue (AR) can vary and does not necessarily reflect profit maximization.

And lastly,
C. Marginal revenue curve is above the average revenue curve: This condition is not directly related to equilibrium. The relationship between MR and AR varies with the type of market structure (e.g., perfect competition, monopoly). In equilibrium, what matters is the relationship between MC and MR, not MR and AR.
