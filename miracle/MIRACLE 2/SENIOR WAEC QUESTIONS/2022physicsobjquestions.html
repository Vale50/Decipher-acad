2.

From the principle of flotation, a body sinks in a fluid until it displaces a quantity of fluid equal to its own

A. Density
B. Mass
C. Weight
D. Volume
The correct answer is Option C. Weight.



**Weight:** According to the principle of flotation (Archimedes' principle), a body will sink in a fluid until it displaces an amount of fluid whose weight is equal to the weight of the body. This principle explains why objects float or sink based on the weight of the fluid displaced.

On the other hand,
**Density:** Density is the mass per unit volume of a substance. While the density of the object relative to the fluid determines whether it will float or sink, it is not the quantity equal to which the fluid is displaced.

Also,
- **Mass:** Mass is the amount of matter in an object. In the context of flotation, it is not directly the displaced quantity of fluid.

And lastly,
- **Volume:** The volume of fluid displaced is equal to the volume of the object submerged, but it is the weight of the displaced fluid that is equal to the weight of the object for it to float.

The length of a simple pendulum is increased by a factor of four. By what factor is its period increased?

A. 0.5 œÄ
B. 2
C. 2 œÄ
D. 4
The correct answer is Option B. 2.



The period of a simple pendulum is given by the formula: \[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} \] where: - \( T \) is the period of the pendulum, - \( L \) is the length of the pendulum, and - \( g \) is the acceleration due to gravity. When the length \( L \) is increased by a factor of four, the new length \( L' \) is: \[ L' = 4L \] Substitute this into the formula for the period: \[ T' = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L'}{g}} = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{4L}{g}} = 2\pi \sqrt{4} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = 2 \cdot 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} = 2T \] This shows that the new period \( T' \) is twice the original period \( T \). Therefore, the factor by which the period is increased is 2.

On the other hand,
**A. 0.5 œÄ**: This option incorrectly suggests that the period would be reduced by a factor involving \(\pi\), which does not follow from the relationship between the period and the length of the pendulum.

Also,
- **C. 2 œÄ**: This option suggests that the period increases by a factor of \(2\pi\), which does not follow from the square root relationship between the period and the length of the pendulum.

And lastly,
- **D. 4**: This option suggests that the period increases by a factor of 4, which does not align with the square root relationship between the period and the length of the pendulum. The period increases by the square root of the factor by which the length is increased, not the factor itself.

The linear expansivity,a , and cubic expansivity,y , of a material are related by the equation

A. y=a
B. y=3a
C. y=2a
D. y=1/3Œ±
The correct answer is Option B. y=3a.



The linear expansivity (\( \alpha \)) and the cubic expansivity (\( \gamma \)) of a material are related by the equation \( \gamma = 3\alpha \). ### Reasoning 1. **Linear Expansivity (\( \alpha \))**: - This is the fractional change in length per degree change in temperature. - Formula: \( \Delta L = L_0 \alpha \Delta T \) - Where: - \( \Delta L \) is the change in length. - \( L_0 \) is the original length. - \( \alpha \) is the linear expansivity. - \( \Delta T \) is the change in temperature. 2. **Cubic Expansivity (\( \gamma \))**: - This is the fractional change in volume per degree change in temperature. - Formula: \( \Delta V = V_0 \gamma \Delta T \) - Where: - \( \Delta V \) is the change in volume. - \( V_0 \) is the original volume. - \( \gamma \) is the cubic expansivity. - \( \Delta T \) is the change in temperature. ### Relationship For a solid material, the change in volume due to temperature is approximately equal to three times the change in length in each dimension. Therefore, the cubic expansivity (\( \gamma \)) is three times the linear expansivity (\( \alpha \)). \[ \gamma = 3\alpha \]

On the other hand,
- **Option A: \( \gamma = \alpha \)** - This implies that the cubic expansivity is equal to the linear expansivity, which is not accurate. The cubic expansivity takes into account expansion in all three dimensions, hence it should be three times the linear expansivity.

Also,
- **Option C: \( \gamma = 2\alpha \)** - This implies that the cubic expansivity is twice the linear expansivity, which is also incorrect. As established, the correct relationship involves a factor of three, not two.

And lastly,
- **Option D: \( \gamma = \frac{1}{3}\alpha \)** - This implies that the cubic expansivity is one-third of the linear expansivity, which is incorrect. The cubic expansivity should be three times the linear expansivity.

A. s t-2
B. s t-1
C. s t
D. s t2.
The correct answer is Option D. s t2.



Here‚Äôs an explanation: When a particle starts from rest and moves with constant acceleration, its displacement \( s \) is related to time \( t \) by the equation of motion: \[ s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \] where: - \( u \) is the initial velocity - \( a \) is the constant acceleration - \( t \) is the time Since the particle is initially at rest, \( u = 0 \). This simplifies the equation to: \[ s = \frac{1}{2}at^2 \] From this equation, it is clear that \( s \) is directly proportional to \( t^2 \), hence \( s \propto t^2 \).

On the other hand,
1. **Option A: \( s \propto t^{-2} \)** This implies that the displacement decreases as the square of time, which is not possible for a particle moving with constant acceleration. In fact, such a relationship doesn't describe motion under constant acceleration at all.

Also,
2. **Option B: \( s \propto t^{-1} \)** This suggests that the displacement decreases inversely with time, which again does not fit the scenario of constant acceleration from rest. This kind of relationship might be seen in other physical phenomena, but not in the context of constant acceleration.

And lastly,
3. **Option C: \( s \propto t \)** This suggests a linear relationship between displacement and time. This would be true if the particle were moving with a constant velocity (not accelerating). However, since the problem involves acceleration, the relationship must be quadratic.

The Joule is equivalent to

A. kg m2 s -1
B. kg2 m s -2
C. kg m2 s -2
D. kg m s -1
The correct answer is Option C. kg m2 s -2.



Here‚Äôs an explanation: A Joule (J) is the unit of energy in the International System of Units (SI). It is defined as the amount of energy transferred when a force of one newton is applied over a distance of one meter. This can be expressed in terms of base units as follows: 1. **Force (Newton)**: A newton (N) is the SI unit of force and is defined as: \[ 1 \, \text{N} = 1 \, \text{kg} \cdot \text{m} \cdot \text{s}^{-2} \] 2. **Work/Energy (Joule)**: Work done (or energy) is calculated as force multiplied by distance. Therefore, the unit of energy (Joule) can be expressed as: \[ 1 \, \text{J} = 1 \, \text{N} \cdot 1 \, \text{m} \] Substituting the unit of force (newton) into this equation: \[ 1 \, \text{J} = (1 \, \text{kg} \cdot \text{m} \cdot \text{s}^{-2}) \cdot 1 \, \text{m} \] \[ 1 \, \text{J} = 1 \, \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^2 \cdot \text{s}^{-2} \] Thus, the Joule is equivalent to \( \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^2 \cdot \text{s}^{-2} \), which matches option C.

On the other hand,
**Option A: \( \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^2 \cdot \text{s}^{-1} \)**: - This unit would be used for a different physical quantity, not energy.

Also,
**Option B: \( \text{kg}^2 \cdot \text{m} \cdot \text{s}^{-2} \)**: - This unit also doesn't match the dimensions of energy and would describe a different physical quantity.

And lastly,
**Option D: \( \text{kg} \cdot \text{m} \cdot \text{s}^{-1} \)**: - This unit corresponds to momentum, not energy.

A. 20 N
B. 25 N
C. 40 N
D. 100 N
The correct answer is Option C. 40 N.



To solve this problem, we need to use the principles of mechanical advantage for a wheel and axle system. Given: - The efficiency of the wheel and axle is 100%. - The ratio of the radii of the wheel to the axle is 5:1. - The mass of the load (\(m\)) is 20 kg. - Gravitational acceleration (\(g\)) is 10 m/s\(^2\). First, calculate the load force (\(F_L\)): \[ F_L = m \cdot g = 20 \, \text{kg} \times 10 \, \text{m/s}^2 = 200 \, \text{N} \] The mechanical advantage (MA) for a wheel and axle is given by the ratio of the radii: \[ \text{MA} = \frac{R_w}{R_a} \] Here, \(R_w\) (radius of the wheel) is 5 times \(R_a\) (radius of the axle): \[ \text{MA} = \frac{R_w}{R_a} = 5 \] Since the efficiency is 100%, the ideal mechanical advantage (IMA) is equal to the actual mechanical advantage (AMA): \[ \text{IMA} = \text{AMA} = \frac{F_L}{F_E} = 5 \] Where \(F_E\) is the effort force required. Rearranging the equation to solve for \(F_E\): \[ F_E = \frac{F_L}{\text{MA}} = \frac{200 \, \text{N}}{5} = 40 \, \text{N} \] **C. 40 N:** This matches the calculated effort force. This should be the correct answer.

On the other hand,
**A. 20 N:** This does not match the calculated effort force based on the given information.

Also,
**B. 25 N:** This also does not match the calculated effort force.

15.

The periodic rise and fall in the intensity of sound produced when two notes of nearly equal frequencies are sounded together is called

A. Doppler effect
B. Beat
C. Interference/div>
D. Resonance
The correct answer is Option A. Doppler effect.



**B. Beat (Correct Answer)** - When two notes of nearly equal frequencies are sounded together, they produce a periodic rise and fall in the intensity of sound. This phenomenon is known as "beats." Beats occur because of the interference between the two sound waves. The alternating constructive and destructive interference causes the sound to oscillate in volume, creating the beat effect.

On the other hand,
**A. Doppler effect (Incorrect)** - The Doppler effect refers to the change in frequency (and wavelength) of a wave in relation to an observer moving relative to the source of the wave. It explains why the pitch of a siren seems to change as it moves past you, but it is not related to the phenomenon of beats.

Also,
**C. Interference (Incorrect)** - While beats are a result of interference, the term "interference" itself is broader and refers to the general phenomenon where two or more waves superpose to form a resultant wave. Interference can be constructive or destructive, but it does not specifically describe the periodic rise and fall in intensity characteristic of beats.

And lastly,
**D. Resonance (Incorrect)** - Resonance occurs when an object or system is driven at its natural frequency, causing it to oscillate with greater amplitude. This is different from the beat phenomenon, which results from the interference of two close frequencies rather than a single frequency being amplified by resonance.
