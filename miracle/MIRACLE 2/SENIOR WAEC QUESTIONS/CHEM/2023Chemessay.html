Describe how iron and aluminium react with each of the following substances; i. dilute H2SO4 ii. Dilute HNO3

Iron (Fe): i. With Dilute H₂SO₄: Iron reacts with dilute sulfuric acid to produce iron(II) sulfate, hydrogen gas, and water. The chemical equation for this reaction is: \[ \text{Fe} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \rightarrow \text{FeSO}_4 + \text{H}_2 \uparrow \] Iron(II) sulfate is a greenish solid that dissolves in water. ii. With Dilute HNO₃: Iron reacts with dilute nitric acid to produce iron(II) nitrate, nitrogen dioxide gas, and water. The chemical equation for this reaction is: \[ \text{Fe} + 2\text{HNO}_3 \rightarrow \text{Fe(NO}_3\text{)}_2 + \text{NO}_2 \uparrow + \text{H}_2\text{O} \] Aluminum (Al): i. With Dilute H₂SO₄: Aluminum reacts with dilute sulfuric acid to produce aluminum sulfate, hydrogen gas, and water. The chemical equation for this reaction is: \[ 2\text{Al} + 3\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \rightarrow \text{Al}_2(\text{SO}_4)_3 + 3\text{H}_2 \uparrow \] Aluminum sulfate is a white solid that dissolves in water. ii. With Dilute HNO₃: Aluminum reacts with dilute nitric acid to produce aluminum nitrate, nitrogen dioxide gas, and water. The chemical equation for this reaction is: \[ 2\text{Al} + 6\text{HNO}_3 \rightarrow 2\text{Al(NO}_3\text{)}_3 + 3\text{NO}_2 \uparrow + 3\text{H}_2\text{O} \] In both cases, the hydrogen gas produced in the reactions is a result of the displacement of hydrogen ions (H⁺) from the acids by the more reactive metals (iron or aluminum). Additionally, the formation of nitrogen dioxide gas in the reactions with nitric acid is due to the oxidation of nitric oxide (NO) produced initially.

i. Equation for the burning of sulfur in air: \[ \text{S} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{SO}_2 \] When sulfur burns in air, it reacts with oxygen to form sulfur dioxide. ii. Catalyst used in the contact process: The catalyst used in the contact process is vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅). iii. Why an excess air is used in the contact process: Excess air is used in the contact process to ensure that there is enough oxygen present for the complete combustion of sulfur dioxide to sulfur trioxide. This maximizes the yield of sulfur trioxide, which is the desired product of the process. iv. Why it is necessary to cool the catalyst used in the contact process: Cooling the catalyst used in the contact process is necessary to prevent it from overheating, which can lead to thermal decomposition or other unwanted side reactions. Cooling helps maintain the stability and effectiveness of the catalyst. v. Reason why the air used in the contact process needs to be as clean as possible: Clean air is important in the contact process because impurities in the air can potentially contaminate the catalyst or interfere with the reaction, reducing its efficiency and effectiveness. Therefore, clean air ensures optimal conditions for the reaction to occur. vi. Reasons why SO₂ should not be discharged into the atmosphere: 1. **Air Pollution:** SO₂ is a major contributor to air pollution and can lead to the formation of sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) in the atmosphere, which contributes to acid rain. Acid rain can harm the environment, ecosystems, and infrastructure. 2. **Health Concerns:** SO₂ can cause respiratory problems, aggravate existing lung diseases such as asthma, and contribute to other health issues. It is classified as a harmful air pollutant by environmental agencies due to its detrimental effects on human health and well-being.
