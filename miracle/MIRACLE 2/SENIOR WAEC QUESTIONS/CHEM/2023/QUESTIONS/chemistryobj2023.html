Which of the following laws or theory cannot be explained by the application of kinetic theory of gases?

A. Dalton’s atomic theory
B.Charles’ law
C. Gay-lussac’s law
D. Boyle’s law
The correct answer is Option A. Dalton’s atomic theory.



**Dalton’s atomic theory** is a theory that explains the nature of atoms and how they combine to form compounds. It includes postulates such as the idea that atoms are indivisible and indestructible, that atoms of a given element are identical in mass and properties, and that chemical reactions involve the rearrangement of atoms. The kinetic theory of gases does not directly explain these postulates.

On the other hand,
**Charles’ Law**: This law states that at constant pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to its temperature. The kinetic theory explains this by stating that increasing temperature increases the kinetic energy of gas molecules, causing them to move faster and occupy a larger volume.

Also,
**Gay-Lussac’s Law**: This law states that at constant volume, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its temperature. The kinetic theory explains this by stating that increasing temperature increases the kinetic energy of gas molecules, causing more frequent and forceful collisions with the container walls, thus increasing pressure.

And lastly,
**Boyle’s Law**: This law states that at constant temperature, the volume of a gas is inversely proportional to its pressure. The kinetic theory explains this by stating that decreasing the volume of a gas increases the frequency of molecular collisions with the container walls, thus increasing pressure.

If 100 cm3? of a saturated solution of sodium tetraoxosulphate (VI) at 30 °C contains 10.5 g of the salt, what would be its solubility at this temperature? [ Na2SO4 = 142]

A.0.57 mol dm-3
B. 0.60 mol dm-3
C. 0.74 mol dm-3
D.2.15 mol dm-3
The correct answer is Option C. 0.74 mol dm-3.



To determine the solubility of sodium tetraoxosulphate (VI) (Na2SO4) at 30°C, we start by calculating the number of moles of Na2SO4 dissolved in 100 cm³ (which is equivalent to 0.1 dm³) of saturated solution. Given: - Mass of Na2SO4 = 10.5 g - Volume of solution = 100 cm³ = 0.1 dm³ - Molar mass of Na2SO4 = 142 g/mol First, calculate the number of moles of Na2SO4: \[ \text{Number of moles} = \frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Molar mass}} = \frac{10.5 \text{ g}}{142 \text{ g/mol}} \] \[ \text{Number of moles} = 0.07394 \text{ mol} \] Next, calculate the solubility of Na2SO4 in mol dm^-3: \[ \text{Solubility} = \frac{\text{Number of moles}}{\text{Volume in dm}^3} = \frac{0.07394 \text{ mol}}{0.1 \text{ dm}^3} \] \[ \text{Solubility} = 0.7394 \text{ mol dm}^{-3} \] Rounding to the appropriate number of significant figures gives us: \[ \text{Solubility} \approx 0.74 \text{ mol dm}^{-3} \] Therefore, the solubility of sodium tetraoxosulphate (VI) at 30°C is \( \boxed{0.74 \text{ mol dm}^{-3}} \).

To determine the nature of the salt solution, we need to consider the properties of the resulting salt and its behavior in water. **Basic**: - Sodium acetate (CH₃COONa) dissociates in water to form sodium ions (Na⁺) and acetate ions (CH₃COO⁻). - The acetate ion (CH₃COO⁻) is the conjugate base of acetic acid (a weak acid). - In water, the acetate ion can hydrolyze (react with water) to form acetic acid and hydroxide ions (OH⁻): \[ \text{CH}_3\text{COO}^- + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{OH}^- \] - The presence of OH⁻ ions makes the solution basic.

**Acidic**: - This option is incorrect because the conjugate acid of the salt (acetic acid) is weak and does not significantly contribute to increasing the H⁺ ion concentration in the solution.

Also,
**Neutral**: - This option is incorrect because a neutral solution would result from a strong acid reacting with a strong base, producing a salt that does not hydrolyze to form either H⁺ or OH⁻ ions. In this case, acetic acid is a weak acid.

And lastly,
**Amphoteric**: - This option is incorrect because an amphoteric substance can act as both an acid and a base. Sodium acetate specifically results in a basic solution, not an amphoteric one.

Which of the following statements about the collision theory is correct?

A. All collisions bring about reactions.
B.Rate of reaction is proportional to the number of effective collisions
C.
D.
The correct answer is Option B.Rate of reaction is proportional to the number of effective collisions.



Collision theory states that for a reaction to occur, reactant particles must collide with sufficient energy and proper orientation. Not all collisions result in a reaction; only those that have enough energy to overcome the activation energy barrier and have the correct orientation will result in a successful or effective collision. Thus, the rate of a chemical reaction depends on the number of these effective collisions per unit time. The more effective collisions there are, the faster the reaction will proceed. Therefore, the statement "Rate of reaction is proportional to the number of effective collisions" is correct according to the collision theory.
